This puzzle is a disguised version of the famously difficult Diophantine equation $a^3 + b^3 + c^3 = 33$. 

For decades, it was an unsolved problem whether 33 could be expressed as the sum of three cubes. It wasn't until 2019 that mathematician Andrew Booker found the first (and only known) integer solution using a massive computer search:
`a = 8866128975287528`
`b = -8778405442862239`
`c = -2736111468807040`

To obscure this equation and render the puzzle immune to simple Google searches or direct LLM recall, I applied a linear transformation to the variables. I chose a unimodular matrix $M$ (determinant 1) so that integer solutions in $(a,b,c)$ perfectly biject to integer solutions in $(u,v,w)$:
`a = 2u + 3v + w`
`b = u + 2v + w`
`c = u + v + w`

Substituting these into $a^3 + b^3 + c^3$ expands exactly to the dense 10-term cubic polynomial seen in the `mystery` function. Because the values involved are 16 digits long, Z3 theorem provers, numerical optimizers, and any brute-force approaches will completely hang or fail. 

To solve the puzzle logically without knowing the secret, a solver would have to compute the Hessian matrix of the cubic form. The determinant of this Hessian factors cleanly into three linear polynomials, revealing the original $a, b,$ and $c$ bases! Once diagonalized, the solver would realize it is the sum of three cubes equal to 33, look up Booker's solution, and apply the inverse matrix:
`u = a - 2b + c`
`v = b - c`
`w = -a + b + c`
yielding the target variables.
